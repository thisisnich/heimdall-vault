## An Effective Negotiator IS

### Research & Analysis
- Willing to research and analyse issues deeply
- Committed to win-wins
- Sensitive to the needs of others

### Conflict Management
- Tolerant of conflict
- Patient
- Able to identify deeper issues

### Preparation
- Prepares and plans well
- Knows his subject
- Thinks reflectively and clearly
- Expresses his thoughts well
- Listens and clarifies

### Strategy
- Prepares thoroughly
- Focuses on interests and needs, rather than positions
- Provides protection and flexibility
- Encourages creative solutions

### Time Management
- Makes time work for him
  - Doesn't let pressure get to him, and has great patience
  - Makes time for his friend when seeking a deal

### Active Listening
- Listens actively
  - Doesn't get distracted by how you feel
  - Builds rapport and demonstrates understanding by reflecting what you are saying and feeling

### Assertiveness
- Asserts your needs
  - Uses assertive language and behavior to state what you think, want, and feel
  - Chooses words carefully and watches body language

### Exploration
- Explores options
  - Increases the number of variables and possibilities for more options for mutual gain

### Verification
- Verifies what has been agreed
  - Goes over every point of agreement by summarising on paper
  - Makes plans to meet/settle future differences

### Relationship Building
- Builds long-term working relationships
  - Is reliable, rational, and receptive, no matter how others behave

### Adaptability
- Adapts his strategy for the situation
  - Thinks win-win all the time!

## Strategy 1: Know Your BATNA

### What is BATNA?
- Stands for 'Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement'

### What it Means?
- Means: 'What is your best backup plan if the answer is no?'

### Why knowing your BATNA helps?
- Knowing your BATNA helps you stay calm, confident, and clear during a negotiation
- It gives you Options → Bargaining Power

---

### How to Use BATNA

#### Scenario 1: Shift Change
You want to change your shift with a colleague to attend a family event. Before asking your supervisor, you think about other options if your request is denied.

#### The BATNA Strategy: 3 Simple Steps

| Step | What It Means | Example |
|------|---------------|---------|
| 1. Think of Your Backup Plan | What will you do if you don't get what you ask for? Look for other choices or solutions. | If you can't get the weekend off, maybe swap with a colleague. Check the shift schedule to see if you can work other shifts. Check company policies about making shift changes. |
| 2. Find Out Your Options | What other choices or useful info can you find? | |
| 3. Use Your Options | Ask with confidence by showing & explaining your options. "I looked at the schedule, and we can change this part to make it work." Shows you have a solution. | |

## Strategy 2: Use Power of Information

### The A.S.K. Framework
Before you ask for something, gather useful facts first. Remember: **A.S.K.**

- **A: Assess their needs**
  - Understand the Preferences, Priorities, and Options of the other party
  - What the other person wants or cares about

- **S: Study their options**
  - Know what choices they have or what they can offer

- **K: Know the norm**
  - Research what other workplaces usually offer for the same kind of job
  - E.g. salary, support, or training opportunities
  - (Schaerer, Teo, Madan, & Swaab, 2020)

---

### Scenario 1: Maintenance Plan Negotiation
Technician John is in a team meeting with the Lead Engineer and other train maintenance crew. He wants to suggest a change in the train maintenance schedule plan as he noticed something important in the train's defects report. John uses the train's detailed breakdown reports over the past 12-month and understands the needs and priorities of the team to explain his idea clearly. This helps everyone agree on the best plan for the train maintenance schedule.

#### How John Applies the Strategy

| Step | How John Applies |
|------|------------------|
| **A: Assess their Needs** | He thinks about what the Lead Engineer and maintenance crew team want (e.g. passenger safety) |
| **S: Study Their Options** | He finds out what corrective actions have been discussed previously (e.g. detailed breakdown reports over the past 12-month). Understand what was done for each breakdown (e.g. what parts were replaced). |
| **K: Know the Norm** | He checks what corrective actions were done for other trains with similar condition. Maintenance polices and manuals with recommended best practices. |

## Strategy 3: Use of Status

- Status based on credentials, professional standing, experience and expertise can influence negotiations by commanding respect and authority (Khakhar & Ahmed, 2017)
- Using your role, experience or skills can help strengthen your voice and influence team decision
- People are more likely to listen and agree with someone who is seen as experienced, confident and in a trusted role
- E.g. a cyber security engineer with Cisco certification and experience dealing with cyber crimes is more likely to influence a team's decision due to his skills and expertise

## Strategy 4: Use of Social Capital

- Social capital involves the trust, friendships and connection you build with people over time
- You can influence a team's decision by asking for help or getting support from people who trust and respect you

**Example:**
Nurse A is in the ICU with a very sick patient. She talks to doctors, therapists, and social workers to plan the best care. Because she has good working relationships with the team, they listen to her, trust her ideas, and work together to help the patient.
